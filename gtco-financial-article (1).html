Unpacking GTCO's Debit-Credit Story

Most people look at a bank statement and see a list. A data analyst looks at the same information and sees a story. This dashboard takes a year's worth of transactions from a GTCO (Guaranty Trust Holding Company) account and asks one simple question: where is the money actually going?

The answer — as you're about to see — is more interesting than the headline numbers suggest. Debits are outrunning credits, cash withdrawals are dominating, and the second half of the year tells a very different story from the first. Let's walk through it, one panel at a time.

Put simply: over this period, ₦356,681 more left the account than came in. That is not a crisis — it is about 2.6% more outflow than inflow. But the direction matters. An account running consistently above 100% debit ratio is drawing down its reserves, not building them.

💡
What does 103% mean in real life? Imagine filling a bucket with water and simultaneously pouring it out through a slightly wider hole. For every ₦100 that came in, ₦103 went out. Over time, that gap compounds — and that's why catching it early with data matters.

Three patterns jump out immediately when you look at this chart:

Pattern 1 — The Mid-Year Surge: Activity climbs steadily from January, peaks sharply in July and August (reaching close to ₦3 million in monthly transactions), then begins to fall. July and August are the high-water marks of this account's activity for the entire year.

Pattern 2 — The October Cliff: Something changed dramatically in October. Both debits and credits fell off a cliff — dropping to a fraction of their July highs. This is not a gradual wind-down; it is a sudden stop. A data analyst should always ask: what happened in October? It could be a change in behaviour, a data cutoff, an account freeze, or a seasonal pattern.

Pattern 3 — Debits always lead: In almost every single month, the debit line sits slightly above the credit line. The gap is small, but it is consistent. This is the behaviour of an account that is always spending slightly more than it earns.

"When two lines travel together for nine months and one consistently hovers above the other, that is not a coincidence — it is a pattern. And patterns in financial data are always telling you something about behaviour."

The 103% Signal — What the Red Ring Is Really Saying

Debit-to-Credit Ratio Gauge
What This Means

The gauge shows debits as a percentage of credits. A healthy account stays at or below 100% — meaning credits cover all debits. At 103%, the account is spending 3% more than it receives.

Think of it like a salary of ₦100,000 but monthly expenses of ₦103,000. Manageable short-term — but unsustainable long-term without an income boost.

🚨
The 3% rule of gradual drain: A 3% overspend sounds small. But applied every month over 12 months, it compounds into a reserve drain of approximately 36% of a single month's income. Small leaks sink big ships.

The pattern is striking: debit transactions outnumber credit transactions by approximately 5-to-1 in most months. This tells us that money is flowing out frequently and in small pieces (consistent with cash withdrawals and airtime), while money comes in less often — likely in fewer, larger credit events like salary payments or transfers.

📊
Frequency vs magnitude: Having 5x more debit transactions than credit transactions is not automatically bad — it just means income comes in bulk while spending is distributed. The concern is when the total debit value also exceeds credit value, which is exactly what we saw in the 103% gauge.

What Should Happen Next — Three Clear Actions

Good analysis always ends with a decision, not just a description. Based on everything this dashboard reveals, here are three concrete next steps:

1. Investigate the October drop immediately. A near-total halt in both debit and credit activity in October is not normal seasonal behaviour. This could reflect a data extraction issue, an account suspension, or a genuine life event. It must be explained before the dashboard can be considered complete.

2. Reduce cash withdrawal dependency. At 65% of all transactions, cash withdrawal is an untracked spending category. Shifting even 20% of this activity to card payments or transfers would give far richer data — and typically correlates with better spending discipline.

3. Set a 100% debit ratio target. The 103% figure needs to be treated as a KPI with a target. Month-on-month tracking of this ratio — visible on the dashboard — would create accountability and make the trend instantly readable to anyone reviewing the account.

✅
The bottom line: This is a healthy, active account with manageable risk — not a crisis. The debit overshoot is small, the volumes are significant, and the data infrastructure (Power BI, clean monthly tracking) is already in place. The next step is simply to act on what the data is already telling you.
